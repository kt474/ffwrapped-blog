# FAAB vs Rolling Waivers

<div class="subtitle">How to choose the right waiver system before the season starts</div>

<div class="author">Written by Kevin Tian</div>

## Introduction

Waiver settings are easy to ignore in June. Nobody has lost a running back to injury yet. Nobody is fighting over the random rookie who suddenly played 72% of snaps. Nobody has spent Tuesday night convincing themselves that this backup tight end is different.

But once the season starts, waivers matter a lot. They decide who gets the breakout players, the injury replacements, the playoff streamers, and sometimes the league winner.

Most fantasy football leagues use either rolling waivers or FAAB. Both work. But if your league is active and wants a little more strategy, FAAB is usually better.

## What Are Rolling Waivers?

Rolling waivers are the classic priority system.

Every manager has a waiver priority. If you have the highest priority and submit a claim, you get the player and move to the back of the line. Everyone else moves up.

It is simple, which is the main benefit. New managers understand it quickly. Commissioners do not have to explain budgets, bids, or tiebreakers. If your league is casual, rolling waivers are perfectly fine.

The problem is that rolling waivers can make managers too passive. If someone has the top priority, they may sit on it for weeks waiting for the perfect player. Meanwhile, other managers have very little chance at the best waiver target unless the top priority decides to pass.

That can feel a little strange. The most important pickup of the season might go to the manager who simply happened to be first in line.

## What Is FAAB?

FAAB stands for Free Agent Acquisition Budget.

Instead of using waiver priority, every manager gets a budget for the season. When players are on waivers, managers place blind bids. The highest bid wins the player and loses that amount from their budget.

For example, if everyone starts with $100, one manager might bid $31 on a breakout running back. Another might bid $18. Another might bid $0 or skip the player entirely. When waivers process, the $31 bid wins.

FAAB is still pretty easy once people have used it for a week or two. The fun part is that every waiver decision has a cost. You can spend aggressively early, save money for later, or try to sneak players through with small bids.

## Why FAAB Is Usually Better

FAAB gives every manager a chance at every player.

That is the biggest reason I prefer it. If a backup running back suddenly becomes a starter, the whole league gets to decide what he is worth. You are not blocked just because someone else has been holding the top waiver priority since draft day.

FAAB also creates more interesting decisions. How much is too much in Week 2? Is a one-week streamer worth $7? Should you save money for the fantasy playoffs? Is the hot waiver name actually useful, or is everyone just reacting to one good game?

Those decisions are part of the game. They reward managers who pay attention, understand their roster, and know when to be aggressive.

It also makes the league feel more fair. You may lose a player because someone bid more than you, but at least you had a real shot.

## Recommended FAAB Settings

For most active redraft leagues, I would use:

- $100 season budget
- Blind bids
- $0 bids allowed
- Waivers processing once per day after games begin
- Free agency available after waivers clear

A $100 budget is easy to understand because every bid feels like a percentage. Bidding $25 means you are spending a quarter of your season budget. A $200 budget works too, but it does not really change the strategy.

I also like allowing $0 bids. If someone is out of money late in the season, they should still be able to make claims. It keeps managers active without letting them beat paid bids.

## Common FAAB Mistakes

The most common FAAB mistake is saving too much money forever.

Managers are often afraid to spend early because they imagine a perfect pickup appearing later. Sometimes that happens. Usually, the best waiver opportunities come when roles change early in the season and there is still time for that player to matter.

The opposite mistake is spending like every Week 1 breakout is a league winner. Some players are worth a big bid. Some are just getting attention because they scored a touchdown on limited snaps.

Before bidding, ask:

- Does this player have a real role?
- Would I start them soon?
- Am I bidding because I need them or because everyone is talking about them?
- How much of my budget will I want later for injuries and bye weeks?

Good FAAB managers are not always cheap. They are intentional.

## So Which Is Better?

For most active fantasy football leagues, FAAB is better than rolling waivers.

It is fairer, more strategic, and more fun once everyone understands it. Every manager gets a shot at every player, and the winner is the person willing to pay the most, not just the person sitting at the top of the priority list.

Rolling waivers are better if your league is casual, beginner-heavy, or allergic to extra settings. They are easy, familiar, and good enough for a lot of leagues.

My recommendation: if your league checks Sleeper regularly and people care about making moves, use FAAB. If half the league only opens the app on Sunday morning, keep rolling waivers and enjoy the peace.

## Conclusion

Waiver settings are a preseason decision that will matter all year.

Drafts get most of the attention, but waiver pickups often decide championships. The breakout player, the injury replacement, the perfect playoff streamer - those players usually come from waivers.

If you want a more competitive league, use FAAB with a $100 budget and $0 bids. If you want the simplest setup, rolling waivers are fine.
