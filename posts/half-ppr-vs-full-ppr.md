# Half-PPR vs Full-PPR: Which Fantasy Football Scoring Format Is Better?

<div class="subtitle">Why half-PPR is the best default for most leagues</div>

<div class="author">Written by Kevin Tian</div>

## Introduction

Choosing between half-PPR and full-PPR sounds like a small commissioner decision. It is only half a point per catch, right?

Over a full season, though, that half point adds up. It changes which players are useful, how managers build their rosters, and whether a five-yard catch feels like a decent play or a scoring event.

Both formats work. I have played in and enjoyed both. But if I were setting up a new fantasy football league today, I would choose half-PPR.

## What Is PPR Scoring?

PPR stands for points per reception.

In full-PPR, a player earns one fantasy point for every catch. In half-PPR, each catch is worth half a point. The receiving yards and touchdowns still count normally.

Take a receiver with six catches for 60 yards:

- Standard scoring: 6 points
- Half-PPR: 9 points
- Full-PPR: 12 points

Nothing about the NFL performance changed. Only the value your fantasy league placed on those six receptions did.

## The Case for Full-PPR

Full-PPR makes more players usable.

Possession receivers, pass-catching running backs, and players who earn a lot of short targets all get a boost. That added depth can be helpful in larger leagues or formats with several flex spots. Managers have more reasonable options when injuries and bye weeks start piling up.

It also creates higher scores, and people generally enjoy watching their fantasy total move. A running back catching a late checkdown in a losing game may not matter much on the field, but it is still nice when your matchup is close.

The downside is that full-PPR can reward catches a little too much. A reception for zero yards is worth the same as a ten-yard run. A receiver who catches eight short passes can outscore a player who was more efficient and created more real offense.

That does not make full-PPR bad. Fantasy scoring has never perfectly measured real football value. It just means receptions can occasionally do more work than they deserve.

## Why I Prefer Half-PPR

Half-PPR keeps most of the benefits of full-PPR without pushing them quite as far.

Players who catch a lot of passes still get rewarded. Pass-catching backs remain valuable. Reliable receivers still have a useful weekly floor. But yardage and touchdowns carry a little more weight.

To me, that balance feels right. A catch should matter, but the player should still need to do something with it.

Half-PPR also gives managers more ways to build a roster. High-volume receivers are valuable, but they do not completely crowd out efficient players or touchdown threats. Running backs who do not catch 70 passes can still keep pace if they are productive on the ground.

It is not a dramatic difference. That is part of the appeal. Half-PPR sits comfortably between the low-scoring, touchdown-heavy feel of standard leagues and the reception-heavy feel of full-PPR.

## How Scoring Changes Draft Value

The format matters most when comparing players with different roles.

Full-PPR gives an extra advantage to:

- Running backs used heavily as receivers
- Slot receivers and short-area target earners
- Players with high catch totals but modest yardage
- Consistent volume over touchdown upside

Half-PPR closes that gap slightly. Big-play receivers, early-down running backs, and efficient players are less dependent on reception volume to compete.

This does not mean you need completely different rankings for each format. The best players are usually still the best players. But close decisions can flip, especially in the middle rounds.

Before your draft, make sure the rankings and mock drafts you use match your league settings. Preparing for full-PPR and then discovering the league uses half-PPR is not a disaster, but it is an avoidable surprise.

## What About Standard Scoring?

Standard scoring gives no points for receptions. It is simple, but it can make fantasy results too dependent on yards and touchdowns.

That tends to shrink the pool of useful players. A receiver can play well, catch seven passes, and still produce a disappointing fantasy score because none of those catches reached the end zone.

Some longtime leagues prefer standard scoring, and there is nothing wrong with keeping a format everyone enjoys. For a new league, though, I would use at least half-PPR. Receptions are part of offensive production and should count for something.

## Which Format Should Your League Use?

My recommendation is straightforward:

- Use **half-PPR** for most new redraft leagues.
- Use **full-PPR** if you want higher scoring, deeper player pools, or several flex spots.
- Keep **standard scoring** if your league already likes its touchdown-heavy style.

If your managers are split, half-PPR is also the easiest compromise. It rewards receiving work without making every short catch feel overly important.

More important than finding the perfect format is choosing one before the draft and making sure everyone knows what it is. Changing reception scoring after managers have drafted would be an impressively efficient way to ruin the league chat.

## Conclusion

Half-PPR and full-PPR can both produce a fun fantasy football season. Full-PPR creates more scoring and gives high-volume pass catchers a larger boost. Half-PPR is a little more balanced.

That is why half-PPR would be my default. Receptions matter, but yards, touchdowns, and efficiency still have room to breathe.
