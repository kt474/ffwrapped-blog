# Fantasy Football League Rules to Decide Before Draft Day

<div class="subtitle">A commissioner checklist</div>

<div class="author">Written by Kevin Tian</div>

## Introduction

Most fantasy football drama does not come from rules everyone agreed on. It comes from rules nobody thought about until they suddenly mattered.

A trade gets vetoed. Someone asks for a lineup mistake to be fixed. Two teams tie for the last playoff spot. A manager stops setting lineups in Week 11. Suddenly the league chat is full of people interpreting a constitution that nobody actually wrote.

The best time to fix that is before draft day. You do not need a 14-page legal document. You just want the boring stuff settled while nobody knows whether a rule helps or hurts their team yet.

These are the fantasy football league rules I would settle before the season starts.

## Scoring Format

Start with scoring, because everything else leans on it.

Make sure everyone knows whether the league is standard, half-PPR, or full PPR. If you use bonuses for long touchdowns, 100-yard games, 300-yard passing games, first downs, or big plays, say that too.

Small scoring settings change how a league feels. Full PPR makes volume receivers and pass-catching backs more valuable. Half-PPR is a nice middle ground. Big-play bonuses add chaos, which can be fun, but only if everyone knows what they signed up for.

Do not assume people clicked through the settings page. Say the important parts out loud before the draft.

## Roster Settings

Roster settings shape draft strategy just as much as scoring, maybe more.

The big decisions are:

- One quarterback or superflex
- Number of RB, WR, and flex spots
- Tight end required or no tight end
- Bench size
- IR spots
- Taxi squad or keeper slots, if relevant

Superflex completely changes quarterback value. Extra flex spots make depth matter more. Deep benches reward stashing upside players. Short benches force managers to make harder waiver decisions.

If you change roster settings from last year, tell people early. Nobody wants to find out during the draft that quarterbacks are suddenly premium assets and their entire mock draft was useless.

## Waiver Rules

Waivers are one of those settings people ignore until the first obvious breakout player hits the wire.

Decide whether you are using FAAB, rolling waivers, or something else. If you use FAAB, choose the budget, whether $0 bids are allowed, when waivers process, and when players become free agents.

My preference for active leagues is FAAB with a $100 budget and $0 bids. It gives every manager a real shot at every player and makes waiver decisions more interesting than sitting on the top priority for six weeks.

If your league is more casual, rolling waivers are fine. Just make the system clear before the first "why did he get that guy?" argument.

For a deeper breakdown, I wrote about [FAAB vs rolling waivers](/posts/faab-vs-rolling-waivers).

## Trade Rules

Trade rules are much easier to discuss before anyone is emotionally attached to a deal.

The biggest question is vetoes. Some leagues let managers vote. Some require commissioner approval. Some only reverse trades if there is clear collusion.

I prefer the last option. Bad trades are part of fantasy football. Collusion is the problem. If every uneven trade becomes a league vote, people start vetoing because a trade helps a rival, not because it is actually unfair.

Set the trade deadline too. I like it late enough to keep the season active, but early enough that eliminated teams are not reshaping the playoffs.

## Playoff Format

Playoff settings deserve more attention than they usually get.

Decide:

- Number of playoff teams
- First-round byes
- Reseeding or fixed bracket
- Tiebreakers
- Whether Week 18 is used

For most leagues, avoid Week 18. NFL teams rest starters, playoff incentives get weird, and fantasy championships can turn into backup roulette.

In a 12-team league, six playoff teams with two byes is common for a reason. Four teams makes the regular season more selective. Eight teams keeps more managers alive, but it can make the regular season feel a little fake if almost everyone gets in.

There is no perfect format. The important thing is picking one that matches how competitive your league wants to be.

## Tiebreakers

Tiebreakers are boring right up until they decide who makes the playoffs.

Set them now. Common options include total points scored, head-to-head record, division record, or all-play record.

I like total points as the first standings tiebreaker because it rewards season-long performance. Head-to-head can be fun, but it is noisy. One weird Week 4 matchup probably should not decide a playoff spot months later.

Whatever you choose, put it somewhere people can see it.

## Keeper Rules

If your league has keepers, write the rules down before the draft. Seriously.

Decide how many players can be kept, what they cost, how long they can be kept, and what happens if a player was drafted, dropped, traded, or picked up from waivers.

Keeper rules get messy fast. The worst version is deciding a keeper edge case after someone realizes they benefit from it.

Even if your league is not a keeper league yet, decide whether future keepers are allowed before this draft. Managers draft differently if they know long-term value matters.

## Commissioner Powers

The commissioner should not be discovering new powers in the middle of the season.

Decide when the commissioner can step in:

- Fixing obvious platform mistakes
- Reversing accidental drops
- Replacing inactive managers
- Pausing or rescheduling the draft
- Handling collusion
- Adjusting lineups after emergencies

You do not need to cover every possible scenario. Just make it clear that commissioner intervention is for obvious problems, not regret.

"I meant to start that player" is usually not an emergency. "Sleeper glitched and duplicated a roster move" might be.

## Payouts and Punishments

If there is money involved, decide payouts before the draft.

First place, second place, regular season winner, highest points, weekly prizes - whatever you want to do, write it down. Do the same for last-place punishments.

This is not because everyone is dishonest. It is because memory gets suspiciously creative once money or embarrassment is involved.

If your league has a punishment, make sure people actually agree to it. A funny punishment nobody will do is just a future argument with better branding.

## Put the Rules Somewhere

Once the rules are decided, put them somewhere easy to find.

Pin them in Sleeper chat. Add them to the league notes. Use a shared doc. It does not matter where, as long as people can find the answer without scrolling through 400 messages from August.

This also makes life easier for the commissioner. Instead of relitigating every issue, you can point to the rule everyone agreed to when nobody had a record yet.

## Conclusion

Good league rules do not make a fantasy football season fun by themselves. But unclear rules can absolutely make one annoying.

Before draft day, decide the settings that matter: scoring, rosters, waivers, trades, playoffs, tiebreakers, keepers, commissioner powers, and payouts.

Once the season starts, the league should be arguing about bad beats, lucky wins, and terrible start-sit decisions. Not whether the rules changed after the fact.
